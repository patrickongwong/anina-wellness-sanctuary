01 Sound familiar?

The "mommy pooch" that won't budge
isn't about willpower.

During pregnancy, the connective tissue running down the middle of your abdomen stretches to make room for your baby. For a great many mothers it doesn't fully knit back together afterward — a condition called diastasis recti (abdominal separation). It is extremely common, it is nothing to be ashamed of, and it is very trainable.

  • A belly that still looks "a little pregnant" months later
  • A ridge or dome down your middle when you sit up
  • Feeling like your core just won't "switch on"
  • Nagging lower-back or pelvic aches through the day
  • A little leaking when you sneeze, laugh, or jump
  • Crunches and planks that feel wrong — or make it worse

02 What's actually happening

Meet the gap:
diastasis recti.

Your "six-pack" muscles run in two columns, joined down the centre by a band of connective tissue called the linea alba. Pregnancy stretches that band — and when it stays loose, the two halves sit apart. That's why the belly can look soft or bulge outward even when you're doing "all the right things."

Here's the hopeful part: closing that gap isn't about tightening from the outside. It's about reconnection — retraining the deep core, pelvic floor and breath to work together again, then loading them gradually until your midline is genuinely strong.

03 Why the usual advice betrays you

More crunches is the worst thing you can do.

Crunches & sit-ups

They pull the two halves of your abs apart under load — often widening the very gap you're trying to close.

Waist trainers & binders

Squeezing from the outside can feel supportive, but it doesn't rebuild the deep strength that actually holds you together.

Straight back to bootcamp

High-impact, high-pressure workouts too soon can overload a core that isn't ready yet — quietly stalling your progress.
